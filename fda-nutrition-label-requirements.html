FDA Nutrition Label Requirements: The Complete Packaging Guide

Quick Summary (TL;DR)

  • The Core Mandate: The FDA requires all packaged foods sold in the US to bear a compliant Nutrition Facts label, displaying accurate % Daily Values based on a 2,000-calorie diet.
  • Typography Constraints: Calories must be bold and highly prominent. The standard minimum font size is 8pt, and fonts must be highly legible sans-serifs (like Helvetica).
  • Allergen Updates: Under the FASTER Act, Sesame is now legally recognized as the 9th major food allergen and must be explicitly declared.

If you are designing packaging for the United States market, your design must survive the strict scrutiny of the U.S. Food and Drug Administration. The FDA does not view the Nutrition Facts label as a design element; they view it as a highly regulated legal document. A single missing nutrient or an improperly formatted allergen warning can trigger a mandatory, multi-million dollar product recall.

In this guide, we break down the exact structural, typographic, and prepress rules you need to follow to guarantee your packaging dielines meet the latest FDA nutrition label requirements.

Official Resource: While this guide serves as a practical blueprint for designers and prepress operators, you should always verify the latest legal thresholds directly via the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) official website.

How to Create an FDA-Compliant Nutrition Label

Execution is everything. To build a label that passes regulatory inspection, you must follow these precise steps in order:

  1. Determine Serving Size: You cannot invent this number. It must be based on the FDA's established Reference Amounts Customarily Consumed (RACC).
  2. Calculate Calories and Nutrients: Determine the exact gram/milligram weight of each nutrient per standard serving size.
  3. List Nutrients in the Required Order: Place them exactly as dictated by the FDA (Total Fat, Saturated Fat, Trans Fat, etc.).
  4. Calculate the % Daily Value (%DV): Divide your serving amount by the FDA's daily limit, then multiply by 100 to get the percentage based on a 2,000-calorie diet.
  5. Format the Layout: Apply the mandatory typographic hierarchy, bolding, and hairline separators.

FDA Nutrition Label Example Breakdown

In 2016, the FDA overhauled the visual structure of the label. As a designer, you must strictly adhere to this exact typographic hierarchy:

  • Servings Per Container: Placed at the very top. Type must be large and highly legible.
  • Serving Size: Directly below servings per container. Must be bolded and reflect realistic consumption amounts.
  • Calories: Must be the largest type size on the entire label and heavily bolded (Minimum 16pt, but often much larger). This is non-negotiable.
  • The % Daily Value Column: Right-aligned. This column tells consumers how much a nutrient in a serving contributes to a daily diet.
  • Added Sugars: Must be indented directly beneath "Total Sugars" in both grams and % Daily Value to differentiate them from naturally occurring sugars.
  • Vitamins & Minerals: Vitamin D and Potassium are now mandatory at the bottom of the label. Vitamins A and C are voluntary.

Common FDA Labeling Mistakes

Avoid these critical errors that trigger immediate rejection by major retailers or FDA warning letters:

  • Incorrect Serving Sizes: Using outdated serving sizes (e.g., calling a 20oz soda 2.5 servings instead of 1 serving).
  • Missing Added Sugars: Failing to indent and declare "Includes Xg Added Sugars."
  • Wrong % Daily Value Calculations: Guessing percentages instead of using the exact FDA mathematical divisor limits.
  • Improper Label Formatting: Scaling the entire vector label proportionally to fit a small space, which illegally shrinks the mandatory 8pt text down to 5pt.

From a Packaging Designer’s Perspective

As a prepress engineer, I see perfectly compliant digital FDA labels get ruined on the actual physical printing press every single day. The rules of digital design do not always translate to the physics of liquid ink.

Print Layout & Dieline Conflicts: Never place your nutrition label closer than 3mm to a score line, fold line, or glue flap on your packaging dieline. When the heavy machinery crushes the cardboard to fold it, the creasing matrix can crack the ink right over your allergen warnings.

The CMYK Text Blur: If you are printing a label on a high-speed flexographic or offset press, never set the tiny 8pt label text using a 4-color Rich Black mix. The vibration of the press will cause the Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, and Black plates to misregister, turning your mandatory legal text into an illegible, blurry smear. Always set your nutrition text to 100% K (Black). You can read more about avoiding this in our 7 Packaging Design Mistakes Guide.

TAC Ink Limits: If you place a white label box over a dark background, ensure the background Total Area Coverage (TAC) does not bleed into your white box. If you fail to trap it correctly, the background colors will flood the label, destroying the contrast and failing legal legibility tests.

Allergen Declaration Rules (FALCPA & FASTER Act)

Food allergy warnings are the most critical safety element on your packaging. The Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act (FALCPA) mandates that labels must clearly identify the food source names of major food allergens.

Recently, the FASTER Act officially recognized Sesame as the 9th major food allergen. The 9 major allergens are: Milk, Eggs, Fish, Crustacean shellfish, Tree nuts, Peanuts, Wheat, Soybeans, and Sesame.

✅ Prepress Pro Tip: Always use a bold "Contains:" statement immediately following the ingredients list (e.g., Contains: Wheat, Milk, and Soy.). Never embed allergens hidden within a dense paragraph of text.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

What are the mandatory nutrients on an FDA label?

The FDA mandates the declaration of Total Fat, Saturated Fat, Trans Fat, Cholesterol, Sodium, Total Carbohydrate, Dietary Fiber, Total Sugars, Added Sugars, Protein, Vitamin D, Calcium, Iron, and Potassium.

What are the FDA allergen declaration rules?

Under FALCPA and the FASTER Act, packaging must clearly state if a product contains any of the 9 major allergens (including Sesame) using a bold "Contains:" statement immediately following the ingredients list.

What is the minimum font size for an FDA nutrition label?

The standard minimum font size is 8pt for core nutrients, 6pt for the bottom footnote, and at least 16pt for the mandatory "Calories" declaration, ensuring high consumer legibility.
