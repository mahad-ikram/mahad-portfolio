FDA Nutrition Label Requirements: The Complete Packaging Guide

Quick Summary (TL;DR)

  • The Core Mandate: The FDA requires all packaged foods sold in the US to bear a compliant Nutrition Facts label, displaying accurate % Daily Values based on a 2,000-calorie diet.
  • Typography Constraints: Calories must be bold and highly prominent. The standard minimum font size is 8pt, and fonts must be highly legible sans-serifs (like Helvetica).
  • Allergen Updates: Under the FASTER Act, Sesame is now legally recognized as the 9th major food allergen and must be explicitly declared.

If you are designing packaging for the United States market, your design must survive the strict scrutiny of the U.S. Food and Drug Administration. The FDA does not view the Nutrition Facts label as a design element; they view it as a highly regulated legal document. A single missing nutrient or an improperly formatted allergen warning can trigger a mandatory, multi-million dollar product recall.

In this guide, we break down the exact structural, typographic, and prepress rules you need to follow to guarantee your packaging dielines meet the latest FDA nutrition label requirements.

Official Resource: While this guide serves as a practical blueprint for designers and prepress operators, you should always verify the latest legal thresholds directly via the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) official website.

The Anatomy of the Modern FDA Label

In 2016, the FDA overhauled the visual structure of the Nutrition Facts label for the first time in over 20 years to better reflect modern eating habits and scientific research. As a designer, you must strictly adhere to this "new" format.

Structural Update Design Requirement Why It Changed
Calories Must be the largest type size on the label and heavily bolded (Minimum 16pt). To immediately highlight the energy value of the product for consumers.
Serving Sizes Must be bolded and reflect realistic consumption amounts (e.g., a 20oz soda is now 1 serving, not 2.5). To stop misleading portion-size mathematics.
Added Sugars Must be indented directly under Total Carbohydrates in both grams and % Daily Value. To differentiate naturally occurring sugars (like in fruit) from processed sweeteners.
Vitamins & Minerals Vitamin D and Potassium are now mandatory. Vitamins A and C are voluntary. Reflects current American dietary deficiencies.

Mandatory vs. Voluntary Nutrients

You cannot simply pick and choose which nutrients look best on your packaging. The FDA dictates a strict tabular order.

Mandatory Nutrients (Must appear in this exact order):

  1. Total Fat
  2. Saturated Fat
  3. Trans Fat
  4. Cholesterol
  5. Sodium
  6. Total Carbohydrate
  7. Dietary Fiber
  8. Total Sugars
  9. Added Sugars
  10. Protein
  11. Vitamin D
  12. Calcium
  13. Iron
  14. Potassium

Note: If a nutrient is zero, it can generally be omitted and listed in a simplified statement at the bottom reading "Not a significant source of..."

Allergen Declaration Rules (FALCPA & FASTER Act)

Food allergy warnings are the most critical safety element on your packaging. The Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act (FALCPA) mandates that labels must clearly identify the food source names of major food allergens.

Recently, the FASTER Act officially recognized Sesame as the 9th major food allergen. The 9 major allergens are:

  • Milk
  • Eggs
  • Fish (e.g., bass, flounder, cod)
  • Crustacean shellfish (e.g., crab, lobster, shrimp)
  • Tree nuts (e.g., almonds, walnuts, pecans)
  • Peanuts
  • Wheat
  • Soybeans
  • Sesame (New)
✅ Prepress Pro Tip: Always use a bold "Contains:" statement immediately following the ingredients list (e.g., Contains: Wheat, Milk, and Soy.). Never embed allergens hidden within a dense paragraph of text.

Typography & Print Production Rules

From a prepress perspective, the FDA label is notoriously rigid. As highlighted in our broader Nutrition Label Compliance Guide, there are strict rules governing how ink hits the paper:

  • Font Selection: You must use a single, highly legible Sans-Serif font. Helvetica and Arial are the industry standards. Decorative or condensed fonts are strictly prohibited.
  • Minimum Font Sizes: Standard text requires a minimum 8pt font. The bottom footnote requires a 6pt minimum.
  • 100% K Black Ink: To avoid common packaging design mistakes, never print the tiny 8pt text of your nutrition label using a 4-color Rich Black mix (CMYK). Commercial offset presses vibrate; printing tiny text in 4 colors causes misregistration and blurry text. Always set label typography to 100% K (Black).

Dual-Column Labels (The Edge Case)

If your package contains between 200% and 300% of the standard reference amount (e.g., a pint of ice cream or a large bag of chips that could be eaten in one sitting or shared), the FDA requires a Dual-Column Label.

The left column must show the nutritional data for a single serving, and the right column must show the data for the entire package. This prevents consumers from doing mental math to figure out how many calories are in the whole bag.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

What are the mandatory nutrients on an FDA label?

The FDA mandates the declaration of Total Fat, Saturated Fat, Trans Fat, Cholesterol, Sodium, Total Carbohydrate, Dietary Fiber, Total Sugars, Added Sugars, Protein, Vitamin D, Calcium, Iron, and Potassium.

What are the FDA allergen declaration rules?

Under FALCPA and the FASTER Act, packaging must clearly state if a product contains any of the 9 major allergens (including Sesame) using a bold "Contains:" statement immediately following the ingredients list.

What is the minimum font size for an FDA nutrition label?

The standard minimum font size is 8pt for core nutrients, 6pt for the bottom footnote, and at least 16pt for the mandatory "Calories" declaration, ensuring high consumer legibility.
